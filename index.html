01 — Profile

A bit about me

I'm working through Level 3 of the Business Management degree, but the corner of business that keeps pulling me in is the screen industries. Specifically the production-management and operations side of them, not the film-making craft. The people I want to be are the ones who coordinate a shoot, keep a schedule on track, watch the budget and keep a production office running. What matters to me professionally is being organised, making decisions I can back with evidence, getting a message to actually land for the people watching, and being straight about how I work. This page pulls together my Level 3 employability work across the modules and the skill sitting behind each piece.

05 — Reflection

Reflection on the page, and what's next

Module: BSM24002 — Academic and Professional Writing and Communication

Reflection (150 words — Jay and Johnson's three-stage reflective typology, 2002)

Standing up for Assignment 1, I presented my own analysis of the Cadbury "Gorilla" advert. Loredana on her own at the front, which felt like a lot at the time. Before I started, all I really had was a feeling that the ad was joyful, and getting from that gut reaction to something I could actually defend was the whole job. When I looked at it again from other angles, it was obvious my opinion wasn't evidence. So I had to learn properly: the difference between emotional brand advertising and the rational, USP-led kind, a bit of semiotics, and backing every claim with Harvard sources, while also weighing up whether selling a sugary product purely on emotion was fair on the viewer. The hard part was organising it like a production schedule across the A2 and A3 clash on 31 July. From here I want to keep evidencing claims and run work to a plan the way a production coordinator has to (Jay and Johnson, 2002).

I've put the 150-word reflection on here too, so the portfolio and the reflection say the same thing. From here I want to:

  • Keep backing every claim with evidence and keep reading the audience, whatever I'm communicating for a production.
  • Keep running work to a schedule the way a production coordinator has to.
  • Find a placement or some volunteering in a film or TV production office to test all of this for real.
  • Plan earlier so two deadlines never land on top of each other again, and keep this page up to date as new work comes through.
06 — BSM24003 · Contexts of the Creative Industries

BSM24003 — Employability Skills Log

This log gathers the employability skills the module asked us to work on, tied to something I actually did. Most draws on two pieces: the Task 1 recorded account on the television and broadcasting industry, and the Task 2 show and tell about my grandparents' vintage television set, a 1960s–70s valve set that's been in the family in Romania for years. My aim is to work on the business and production-management side of film and television, so under each skill I've said how it would carry over into running a production office or keeping a shoot on track.

Skill 1

Digging into an industry and making sense of it

Task 1 meant getting to grips with where television came from: early radio and the founding of the BBC, the post-war boom, ITV and the split between the licence fee and advertising, cable and satellite, the digital switchover, and now streaming. All onto a dated timeline with a note of where each fact came from.

Evidence in the logmy research notes, the source list I built, and a screenshot of the broadcasting timeline I made for the slides.

Skill 2

Talking it through and presenting

Two spoken pieces against the clock: the broadcasting account in ~10 minutes to camera, the television-set talk in ~5 in the room. For each I wrote a running order, rehearsed until the timing fit, and worked off slides and the photo of the set rather than reading word for word.

Evidence in the loga still from the recorded video, one slide from the broadcasting deck, the photo of the vintage television set, and the upload link.

Skill 3

Working the digital and production-admin side

I recorded and exported the Task 1 video myself, built the slide deck, and built this log in the browser. I also looked at the scheduling and production-admin tools a production office leans on — call sheets and shoot schedules — because that's the work I'm aiming for.

Evidence in the loga grab of the recording and editing tool, a screenshot of the Padlet build view, and a simple schedule I drew up.

Skill 4

Keeping myself on track to the deadlines

A fair bit running at once. I got the Task 2 write-up onto Connect the day before the talk, then kept Task 1 and this log moving towards the Week 12 hand-in. What stopped it slipping was breaking the work into smaller jobs and treating rehearsal slots as fixed appointments.

Evidence in the loga screenshot of my planner and the Connect submission confirmation.

Skill 5

Reflecting, and thinking about the ethics and the money

The show and tell got me thinking about what an old family object holds onto, and how that set made me curious about how programmes get made and reach people. The Task 1 research pushed me into television's harder questions: whether the licence fee survives and how public-service broadcasting gets funded, what streaming is doing to audiences and to the money, who gets represented on screen, fair pay and how shaky the work can be, and the energy cost of production and streaming.

Evidence in the logthe photo of the vintage television set with a short caption, and a screenshot of my Task 1 "current challenges" slide.

Reflection — Greenaway's Do–Review–Plan–Apply cycle

Do — my two main pieces were the recorded account on the television and broadcasting industry and the show and tell on my grandparents' vintage television set. Review — I researched broadcasting and built a cited timeline, planned and recorded the ten-minute account, then prepared and gave the five-minute talk, building the slides and log digitally and keeping the staged deadlines in hand; the harder bits were pacing to time and steadying my nerves on camera. Plan — I can now evidence five things: research and contextual analysis; spoken and visual communication; digital and production competence; self-management; and reflective, ethical and commercial awareness; presenting is the one I most want to strengthen. Apply — these feed into my goal of a production-coordinator role, where you research, communicate, run schedules and admin, hit deadlines, and produce responsibly.

BSM24001 — Employability Skills Log

I'm Loredana Stefan, a Business Management student aiming at the production-management side of film and TV. The five skills below come out of this module's MCQ quiz (Task 1) and our loot-box debate (Task 2).

Part A — five skills

Skill 1

Working independently and self-direction

No tutor set my revision timetable for the in-class quiz. Early in the module I carved the five concept areas into my own list — creative process and divergent thinking, cognitive biases, the business decision tools, logical reasoning and perspective-taking — and worked through them at my own pace. By the 45-minute paper I sat down knowing I'd put the work in. That self-starting is what a production coordinator needs when no one is chasing the schedule.

Skill 2

Organising and prioritising under deadline

The quiz revision and the debate prep both fell due inside the same module window. I mapped them out like a shoot schedule and worked out the order. Nothing got shoved to the last night. It's the same juggling I do drawing up a call sheet for a student film.

Skill 3

Persistence and following instructions

The free-to-play funding argument was my case against the motion, and my first draft of it didn't survive once I held it up to the evidence. So I reworked the argument cards, forcing each one into the brief's claim, evidence and reasoning shape until they stood up, then delivered the exact section my group had assigned me. They got something they could count on.

Skill 4

Willingness to learn new tools and methods

The debate pushed me into Harvard referencing and proper source-checking. I had to sort peer-reviewed loot-box studies and regulator documents from opinion pieces, the same checking I do verifying details on a shoot. By the end I could cite credible academic and industry sources without second-guessing.

Skill 5

Managing pressure and composure

The 20-minute debate had me nervous, and with the quiz sat right on top the week felt tight. What got me through was running my lines in short bursts and slicing the prep into bits small enough that it never built into a wall. On the day I delivered my half of the loot-box motion cleanly and inside the time.

Module: BSM24001 — Critical and Creative Thinking

Part B — Reflective Summary (Bassot's Integrated Reflective Cycle, 2013)

Situation: I completed an MCQ quiz, prepared and delivered a debate on loot-box monetisation under time pressure, and managed a student film shoot schedule simultaneously.

Reaction & Reflection: During the debate, an opposing speaker challenged my point on free-to-play funding. I had to adapt quickly and reorder my arguments. Afterwards I realised that counter-arguments were my weak area and that nerves affected my preparation. This highlighted the need for better planning and composure.

Learning: I learned the value of the module's claim-evidence-reasoning structure and source-checking. These tools strengthened my arguments and are essential for credible communication in production-business work.

Development & Application: Moving forward, I will use structured planning, strengthen my argument skills, and practice handling pressure. I will apply these skills in future modules and production projects to improve confidence and effectiveness.

Reference Bassot, B. (2013) The Reflective Practice Guide. Abingdon: Routledge.
